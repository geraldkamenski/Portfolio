Thermal Performance Evaluation of a Compact Air to Liquid Assisted GPU Cooling Module

Background

This project was developed to address thermal inefficiencies commonly observed in multi graphics card systems, where one graphics card receives preheated exhaust air from another, leading to reduced performance and reliability. To mitigate this issue, a pre intake cooling module was designed to condition incoming air using a liquid cooled heat transfer plate and a driven impeller, lowering air temperature before it enters the graphics card and improving overall thermal efficiency.

Goal

Simulate heat transfer from hot intake air to a liquid cooled cold plate upstream of the impeller.

Simulation Results

Air Inlet to Impeller Heat Map

  • We can see that inlet air temperature (set to 140 °F) is cooled by liquid cold plate.
  • Cold spots at impeller are due to the idealized outlet volume flow boundary condition, which artificially accelerates fluid and reduces local static temperature due to energy redistribution in the solver.
  • Does not consider rotating forces of the impeller.
  • Does not consider the heat output of a small motor driving the impeller.

Conclusion

The simulation demonstrates that an air to liquid assisted cooling module is capable of reducing inlet air temperature by approximately 10 °F from its initial air inlet temperature (140 °F) under the modeled operating conditions. The liquid cold plate module introduces 68 °F water into the system, extracting heat from the incoming hot air before it is directed upward by the impeller. Overall, the results indicate that the concept is thermally viable and capable of delivering measurable air temperature reduction upstream of the GPU intake. Future work would include incorporating rotating impeller physics, motor heat generation, and transient operating conditions to further refine predicted performance.
